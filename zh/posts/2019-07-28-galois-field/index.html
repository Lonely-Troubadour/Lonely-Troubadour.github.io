Group Theory

Group Theory is the fundamental tool for studying symmetry and the foundation of cryptography. Here,

Definition and examples

DEFINITION 1.1

A group is a set $G$ together with a binary operation $(a,b) \mapsto a * b:G \times G \rightarrow G$ satisfying the following conditions:

G1: (associativity) for all $a,b,c \in G$,

$$(a * b) * c = a * (b * c)$$

G2: (existence of a neutral element) there exists an element $e \in G$ such that

$$ a * e = a = e * a $$

G3: (existence of inverses) for each $a \in G$, there exists an $a' \in G$ such that $$a * a' = e= a' * a$$ .

Usually, we abbreviate $(G, *)$ to $G$. Also, we usually write $ab$ for $a * b$ and 1 for $e$; alternatively, we write $a + b$ and $a * b$ and 0 for $e$. In the first case, the group is said to be multiplicative, and the second is said to be additive.

DEFINITION 1.2

In the following, $a, b \dots$ are elements of a group $G$.

(a) An element $e$ is a neutral element if satisfying G2. If $e'$ is a second such element, then $e' = e * e' = e$. In fact, $e$ is the unique element of $G$ such that $e * e = e$ (apply G3).

(b) If $b * a = e$ and $a * c = e$, then $b = b * e = b * (a * b) = (b * a) * c = e * c = c$

Hence the element $a'$ in (G3) is uniquely determined by $a$. We call it the inverse of $a$, and denote it $a^{-1}$ (or the negative of $a$, and denote it $-a$).

(c) Note that (G1) shows that product of any ordered triple $a_{1}, a_{2}, a_{3}$ of elements of $G$ is unambiguously defined: whether we form $a_{1}a_{2}$ first and then $(a_{1}a_{2})a_{3}$, or $a_{2}a_{3}$ first and then $a_{1}(a_{2}a_{3})$, the result is the same. In fact, (G1) implies that the product of any ordered $n-$tuple $a_{1}, a_{2}, a_{3}, \dots, a_{n}$ of elements of $G$ is unambiguously defined. We prove this by induction on $n$. In one multiplication, we might end up with $(a_{1} \dots a_{i})(a_{i+1} \dots a_{n})$ as final product, whereas in another we might end up with $(a_{1} \dots a_{j})(a_{j+1} \dots a_{n})$

Note that the expression within each pair of parentheses is well defined because of the induction hypotheses. Thus if $i = j$, they are equal. If $i \neq j$, we may suppose $i < j$. Then $(a_{1} \dots a_{i})(a_{i+1} \dots a_{n})=(a_{1} \dots a_{i})((a_{i+1} \dots a_{j})(a_{j+1} \dots a_{n}))$ $(a_{1} \dots a_{j})(a_{j+1} \dots a_{n})=((a_{1} \dots a_{i})(a_{i+1} \dots a_{j}))(a_{j+1} \dots a_{n})$ and the expression on the right are equal because of (G1).

(d) The inverse of $a_{1}a_{2} \dots a_{n}$ is $a_{n}^{-1}a_{n-1}^{-1} \dots a_{1}^{-1}$

(e) (G3) implies that the cancellation laws hold in groups, $$ab = ac \Longrightarrow b = c, \qquad ba = ca \Longrightarrow b = c $$ (multiply on left or right by $a^{-1}$). Conversely, if $G$ is finite, then the cancellation laws imply (G3): the map $x \mapsto a x: G \rightarrow G$ is injective, and hence (by conunting) bijective; in particular, $e$ is in the image, and so $a$ has a right inverse; similarly, it has a left inverse, and the argument in (b) above shows that the two inverses are equal.

$\quad$ Two groups $(G, *)$ and $(G', *')$ are isomorphic if there exists a one-to-one correspondence $a \leftrightarrow a'$,$G \leftrightarrow G'$, such that $(a * b)' = a' *' b'$ for all $a, b \in G$.

$\quad$ The order $|G|$ of a group $G$ is its cardinality. A finite group whose order is a power of a prime $p$ is called a $p$-group.

$\quad$ For an element $a$ of a group $G$, define

\[ a^{n} = \left \{ \begin{aligned} &(aa \dots a), \quad {n > 0 \quad (n \text{ copies of } a)} \\ &e, \quad {n = 0} \\ &(a^{-1}a^{-1} \dots a^{-1}), \quad {n < 0 \quad (|n| \text{ copies of } a^{-1})} \end{aligned}\right. \]

一个群是一个集合$G$带有一个“二元运算”，即一个映射$G \times G \to G$（称为乘法,$(a,b) \in G \times G$在此映射下的像记作$a \bullet b$或$ab$，称为$a$与$b$的积），满足条件：

  1. $(ab)c=a(bc)$对任意$a,b,c, \in G$成立（这称为“乘法结合律”）；
  2. 存在（唯一的）$e=e_{G} \in G$使得$ae=ea=a$对任意$a \in G$成立（$e$称作单位元）；
  3. 对任意$a \in G$，存在（唯一的）$b \in G$使得$ab=ba=e$（$b$称作$a$的逆元，记$b=a^{-1}$。
